Cost & Money

A basic life insurance policy can cost less than what most people spend on data or lunch per week. The question isn't really whether you can afford it — it's whether your family can afford for you not to have it. And the longer you wait, the more expensive it gets. Starting small now is always better than waiting for the "right time" that never comes.

Savings deplete. A prolonged illness before death can drain everything before the policy even pays out. And most people's savings aren't large enough to replace years of income for a family. Life insurance is not a substitute for savings — they serve different purposes. One is for living, the other protects against the financial damage of dying too soon.

A savings account gives you access to what you put in. Life insurance gives your family access to the full coverage amount from day one — even if you've only paid one premium. If you put $50,000 in a savings account and die, your family gets $50,000. If you have a $5 million policy and die after one payment, your family gets $5 million. That leverage is what makes it fundamentally different.

Even a modest payout can cover a mortgage for a year, clear outstanding debt, or fund a child's education. It doesn't have to replace a lifetime of income to make a meaningful difference. The right coverage amount is a conversation, not a guess — and it's usually more affordable than people expect to get meaningful protection.

A common starting point is ten times your annual income, but it really depends on your debts, your dependents, your lifestyle, and your goals. A needs analysis with an advisor takes about 20 minutes and gives you a specific number based on your actual situation rather than a generic estimate.

Family & Work Situations

The income stops, but the bills don't. Rent or mortgage, school fees, utilities, groceries, loan payments — all of that continues. Most families either go into debt, sell assets, or depend on relatives. A life insurance payout doesn't replace you, but it buys your family time to breathe and rebuild without financial collapse on top of grief.

Family support is real and valuable. But consider what you're actually asking of them — to financially absorb your death on top of grieving it. That strain breaks families. Life insurance means your passing doesn't also become a financial burden on the people who love you most.

Think about what a stay-at-home parent actually does — childcare, cooking, household management, school runs. If that person dies, the surviving spouse has to pay for all of those services while still working. The economic value of a non-earning spouse is real. Losing them without coverage creates a financial hole most families don't anticipate.

Group life through an employer is better than nothing, but it has serious limitations. It typically only covers you while you're employed there. If you resign, get laid off, or the company closes, that coverage is gone. You also usually have no control over the coverage amount. Personal life insurance is yours regardless of where you work.

Self-employed people actually need life insurance more than salaried workers. There's no HR, no group plan, no employer safety net. If you stop working, the income stops immediately. A policy ensures that if something happens to you, your business debts and personal obligations don't collapse onto your family.
